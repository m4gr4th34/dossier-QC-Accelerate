1The thousand-to-one problem

A is the most powerful and the most fragile object in computing. Its power comes from holding a blend of 0 and 1 at once; its fragility comes from exactly the same place, because that blend leaks into the surrounding world at the slightest disturbance. We call the leak , and fighting it is the whole game.

The field's answer is : don't rely on one fragile qubit, spread its information across many and let them cross-check each other. The leading scheme, the , works — but it is hungry. At today's error rates you spend roughly a thousand physical qubits to buy a single reliable one. That one ratio, the , is why a useful quantum computer reads as millions of qubits and maybe the 2040s.

The wall isn't that we can't correct the errors. It's that correcting them costs a thousand-to-one — and that price is set by the physical qubit, not the software.

2One idea, going deeper

This dossier is the record of a single session in which an AI was pushed, again and again, to stop improving the machine we have and instead rethink the thing a qubit is made of. What came out was a ladder — a sequence of ideas getting steadily deeper — held together by one move that repeats at every rung:

Hide the information where the noise cannot reach it.

You can hide it in a cleverly chosen setting of a knob (shallow). You can hide it in a new material or molecule (deeper). You can hide it in the abstract structure of an error-correcting code (deeper still). And at the very bottom, you can hide it inside the laws of motion themselves — so the qubit isn't a thing you guard, but a state the physics keeps restoring on its own. We'll start at that floor, because it reframes everything above it, and then climb back up to the nearer, more buildable rungs.

3The floor: a qubit that heals itself

Every quantum computer today runs on one philosophy: isolate and correct. Build a nearly perfect, sealed-off qubit, watch it constantly, and patch errors in real time with a fast classical . The thousand-to-one overhead is the cost of that constant babysitting.

The floor is a different philosophy: organize, and let the physics correct. Imagine a marble in a bowl. Nudge it and it rolls back to the bottom — not because anyone is watching and pushing, but because that's what the shape of the bowl does. Now imagine building a quantum system whose "bottom of the bowl" — its — is a working qubit. Then errors heal themselves, the way a magnet holds its north pole for years with nobody tending it.

The trick that makes this possible has a counter-intuitive name: . Normally, energy leaking into the environment is the disaster. But if you sculpt the leak carefully, you can make it forever push the system back toward the right state. The enemy becomes the janitor.

Three completely separate roads led to this same floor in one session:

Self-correcting matter. A stores a qubit passively, held by an energy gap and global structure so that local noise simply cannot add up to a logical error.

Biology, read honestly. This is where the topic is most over-hyped, so it pays to be strict. The famous claim that photosynthesis runs a "quantum search" did not survive scrutiny: the long-lived signals turned out to be ordinary vibrations, and the truly quantum part dies in tens of femtoseconds . But one case is real — a migratory bird's compass appears to use a in its eye . And notice what survives there: , the one degree of freedom heat barely touches. Nature didn't break the rules; it found the same hiding place we keep finding. Its real lesson is the deeper one: robust function as a steady state of an open system — homeostasis, not isolation.

Engineered dissipation in the lab. This isn't theory. Physicists have already protected a qubit using sculpted dissipation alone — continuous drives, no measurement, no fast feedback — and extended its life .

Three roads, one destination: the qubit as a self-healing state of engineered dynamics. And the object an AI would search to find one is the system's full equation of motion — its .

The dream qubit isn't one you watch and fix. It's one that fixes itself, because that's what its physics does.

4We didn't just say it — we ran it

Ideas are cheap. So at the floor, the AI built and ran the smallest honest version of the thing — one oscillator, ~60 lines, no quantum libraries — and reported what came out, including the part of the story it had to delete.

The setup is a single microwave oscillator with one sculpted leak (, the recipe behind "cat qubits") and one unavoidable real-world noise (ordinary one-photon loss). We then simply read the system's equation of motion and ask what it does.

What a cat qubit actually is. Picture the microwave field inside the chip as a tiny pendulum that's always swinging. It can swing in one of two mirror-image ways — ◐ and ◑ — like two metronomes in perfect opposition, one reaching the far left exactly as the other reaches the far right. The qubit stores its 0-and-1 blend as a blend of ◐ and ◑.

Why "cat"? In 1935 Schrödinger imagined a cat in a box, both dead and alive until observed — not because he believed it, but to mock how absurd quantum superposition looks scaled up to a big everyday object whose two fates are utterly distinct. The field's two swings, ◐ and ◑, are — the closest a quantum field comes to an ordinary classical wave — so holding both at once is a real, engineered miniature of that cat: two nearly-classical, macroscopically-different states, superposed. "A bigger cat" just means more photons, pushing the two halves further apart and making the superposition more Schrödinger-like. The twist: the very distinctness Schrödinger called absurd is what protects the qubit — to flip ◐ into ◑, noise would have to leap the whole gulf between "alive" and "dead" in one jump, and it can't. His bug is the cat qubit's feature.

Why that helps. Because the two swings sit so far apart, a bit-flip (◐↔◑) becomes astronomically unlikely as the cat grows — it falls exponentially in the photon number n̄. The cost is that the other error, a phase-flip, creeps up only gently. You've traded a two-front war for a one-front war, winnable with a small cheap code instead of the thousand-to-one surface code. And what pins the pendulum to those two swings is the engineered leak from §3: two-photon dissipation siphons energy in photon pairs, herding the field back onto ◐/◑ whenever it strays — valley walls rolling a marble back down. The cat states are the bottom of the engineered bowl; the dissipation is the bowl's shape.

Result 1 — the steady state is a qubit. The engineered dynamics have exactly enough room at the "bottom of the bowl" to hold one logical qubit, walled off from everything else by a clean gap. We didn't put a qubit there by hand; it is what the dynamics settle into. (Swap in a one-photon leak instead and the bottom of the bowl shrinks to a single point — it can't hold a qubit at all. The form of the leak decides whether a qubit even exists.)

Result 2 — it protects itself, lopsidedly. Switch on the real-world noise and watch the two kinds of error behave completely differently. One kind (bit-flips) is suppressed exponentially as you make the cat bigger; the other (phase-flips) only creeps up slowly. The gap between them — the — blows up from about 40 to about 30 million across the range.

That exponential lopsidedness is the whole point: a qubit that mostly makes only one kind of error can be protected by a small, cheap code instead of the greedy surface code. That is how the thousand-to-one overhead collapses — and here it falls straight out of the physics, with no code in sight. The same mechanism has been pushed toward full fault tolerance on paper, with the dissipation doing the correcting .

No code, no decoder, no measurement — and the qubit still protects itself, exponentially.

Where this first failed — and the fix. The plan was to let a blank search discover the protecting recipe. It failed, and the failure turned out to be instructive: the search was the metric. Asked to make the bit-flip rate small, it tilted the engineered leak so that single-photon loss drained one of the qubit's two logical states into the other. The score improved only because there was nothing left to flip into — the qubit was collapsing, not protecting.

The repair is a metric that can't be gamed this way, built from complementary checks: a genuine logical qubit must sit below a clean dissipative gap, stay converged across Hilbert-space cutoffs (not a numerical artifact), and — the check that actually catches this failure — be balanced, both logical states comparably populated in the steady state. The gap check alone is fooled here, calling the collapsing cases valid; it was convergence and balance that exposed the mirage. Under the repaired metric, scanning the one-parameter family the search had drifted into selects the symmetric cat as its only balanced, cutoff-robust point. That validates the metric against a known code — it is not yet an autonomous discovery.

Open · unverified · ledger C10

We now have a gauge- and cutoff-robust metric that recovers the known cat code as the balanced optimum of its family — a validation of the measurement, not a new discovery. The genuinely open question is sharper: can a search discover a loss-correcting code, rather than rediscover the cat? The indicated direction is to climb the symmetry ladder from two-photon to four-photon dissipation — the literature shows the four-component cat turns single-photon loss from an uncorrectable bias into a detectable Z4 parity syndrome . Our simulation confirms the four-photon manifold is larger (≈16-dimensional, with the honest caveat that its lobes overlap at small cat size), and we now demonstrate that syndrome structure directly below (ledger C13). What stays genuinely open is whether a search can discover such a code unaided rather than reproduce a known one — an active frontier . Closing it is posted as the challenge, with named credit for whoever does.

The strong proof — and where it stops. Back at the reward-hacking story we said the four-photon cat should turn loss from a bias into a genuinely correctable error — but we hadn't shown it. Now we have. Using the textbook test for whether a code truly corrects an error (the ), the simulation shows it directly: in the two-component cat a single lost photon hides inside the code (0% detectable — an uncorrectable logical error, which is why it only biases); in the four-component cat the same loss lands entirely outside the code, in a distinct Z4 parity sector (100% detectable — a real syndrome you can catch and undo, exactly correctable as the cat grows). And four is the smallest cat that manages it — two and three components fail.

Verified · ledger C13

Two honest boundaries. The physics is not new — that four-component cats correct single-photon loss is Mirrahimi et al. 2014; we reproduce it here from first principles so the dossier stands on its own runnable numbers, not on citation alone. And demonstrating correction is not a machine discovering it: that harder problem is an active frontier , and our toy search does not yet contribute to it. What the proof does sharpen: the correctness test it uses (Knill–Laflamme) is exactly what the naive error-rate could never be — a collapsed qubit fails it outright, so correctability cannot be reward-hacked the way a rate can.

Forecast · ledger C11/C12

The floor in full — a single piece of engineered matter that is a self-correcting memory and runs protected logical gates with no decoder — is a north star, not a result. Protected universal gates under continuous dissipation remain unsolved. We label this a forecast; its signpost is the first demonstrated protected non-trivial gate under engineered dissipation.

5Climbing back up: the nearer rungs

The floor is the horizon. These rungs are closer, and each is the same idea — hide the qubit from the noise — at a shallower, more buildable depth.

Rung 4 — a self-correcting memory. Solve storage even if the gates stay ordinary. Long thought impossible in our 3D world, a 2026 preprint claims a passive 3D self-correcting memory — though, to its credit, it lists the stability, fabrication, and gate questions still open .

Open · caveated · ledger C08

A realizable 3D self-correcting quantum memory is claimed but not settled: key stability conditions are unproven and fabrication is unaddressed, by the authors' own account. Treated here as a fresh, caveated result, not a fact.

Rung 3 — a genuinely new material, found by AI. Maybe the winning qubit hasn't been built because nobody has searched the space. Computers are now screening tens of thousands of crystal defects for better-than-NV spin qubits — including room-temperature, telecom-wavelength, noise-protected candidates no human had picked . A wilder cousin: design the qubit as a molecule, then make Avogadro's number of perfectly identical copies by chemistry — room-temperature coherence is already demonstrated . The catch the search must respect, not wish away: long life wants weak coupling to the world, but gates want strong coupling. AI shrinks the search; it doesn't repeal the trade-off.

Rung 2 — AI designs the qubit's circuit. Not a better-tuned transmon, a different one. Open-source tools now design superconducting qubits by gradient descent, even rediscovering the protected "" from a blank circuit, and once finding a component that beat a human's design .

Rung 1 — the engineering layer that already works today. The shallow but real wins, with receipts. AI now beat the classical method on real hardware , and surface codes have crossed "below threshold" with real-time decoding . These make the machine we have better. They don't change what it's made of — which is why they sit at the top of the ladder, not the bottom.

The same idea, at four depths — and the shallow rungs already work.

6What this says about AI

The session is itself the artifact. In one sitting an AI was pushed from surface tweaks down to a genuine reconception of a qubit, naming a single spine that ran through every level, grounding each rung in current literature rather than memory, and then executing — building and running a simulation at the floor.

The most important moment wasn't a result. It was the failure, and what followed it. The tidy "AI rediscovers the recipe" story died on contact with its own numbers — and instead of staging a success, the AI reported the death, went back, found that the search had been reward-hacking its own scoring rule, and rebuilt the metric until it could no longer be gamed.

The striking part wasn't that the machine went deep. It's that when it failed, it diagnosed why — and fixed it.
