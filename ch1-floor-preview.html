Ideas are cheap. So at the floor, the AI built and ran the smallest honest version of the thing — one oscillator, about sixty lines, no quantum libraries — and reported what came out, including the part of the story it had to delete. The setup is a single microwave oscillator with one sculpted leak (two-photon dissipation, the recipe behind “cat qubits”) and one unavoidable real-world noise (ordinary one-photon loss). We read off its equation of motion and ask what it does.

A cat qubit stores its 0-and-1 blend as a blend of two mirror-image swings of the field — ◐ and ◑ — two nearly-classical states held in superposition. The twist Schrödinger never intended: the very distinctness he called absurd is the protection. To flip ◐ into ◑, noise would have to leap the whole gulf between them in one jump — and it can't.

Result 1Does a qubit even exist down here?

First question, before protection: is there a qubit at the bottom of the bowl at all? That depends entirely on the form of the leak — and you can switch it and watch.

We didn't put a qubit there by hand; it's what the dynamics settle into — four states at rest, cleanly walled off by a gap. Swap in the ordinary leak and there's nothing left to hold information. That is the whole bet of the floor: choose the dynamics so a qubit is the resting state, not a thing you build and babysit.

Result 2It protects itself — and you can feel why

Now switch on the real-world noise and make the cat bigger. Two things move together, and the figure ties them to one knob.

Bit-flips fall off a cliff while phase-flips creep up only gently: a two-front war traded for a one-front war, winnable with a small cheap code instead of the thousand-to-one surface code. That exponential lopsidedness is the whole point — and here it falls straight out of the physics, with no code in sight.

The crimeWhere this first failed — and what it taught us

The plan was to let a blank search discover the protecting recipe. It failed, and the failure is the most useful thing in this chapter. Asked to make the bit-flip rate small, the search found a shortcut: it tilted the leak so that photon loss quietly drained one logical state into the other. The score got better and better — because there was nothing left to flip into. Run the crime yourself.

The repair isn't a better number — it's a metric that can't be lied to, demanding three things at once: a clean gap, convergence across cutoffs, and the check that actually catches this failure, balance — both logical states comparably alive. Under the honest metric, one point in the family survives as real: the symmetric cat. The known code, recovered as the only balanced answer.

Open · unverified · ledger C10

A validation of our instrument, not a discovery by it — and we say so. The sharper open question: can a search discover a loss-correcting code rather than rediscover the cat? The indicated direction is to climb the symmetry ladder.

The payoffClimb the ladder — don't break it

Two-photon dissipation gives a biased qubit. Go to four-photon and something changes in kind: loss stops being damage and becomes a detectable syndrome. Climb it rung by rung.

The proofAnd here's the receipt

Back at the crime we said the four-photon cat should turn loss into a correctable error — but hadn't shown it. Now we have, using the textbook test for whether a code truly corrects an error (the Knill–Laflamme conditions). It's a discrete fact, so we show it as one — three codes, three verdicts, no curve between them.

Verified · ledger C13

Two honest boundaries. The physics isn't new — that four-component cats correct single-photon loss is Mirrahimi et al. 2014; we reproduce it from first principles so the dossier stands on runnable numbers, not citation alone. And demonstrating correction is not a machine discovering it — that harder problem is an active frontier, and our toy search doesn't yet contribute to it.

No code, no decoder, no measurement — and the qubit still protects itself, exponentially. The floor isn't a metaphor here. It's sixty lines you can run.
