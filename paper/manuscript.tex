\begin{abstract}
\noindent AI has quietly become a co-author of quantum-computing research, contributing
across the stack in two celebrated modes: \emph{re-deriving} what humans designed (faster
decoders, better pulses) and \emph{discovering} what humans did not (new error-correcting
codes). This chapter demonstrates a third, quieter mode---\emph{scoping}: using an AI search,
constrained by a deliberately un-gameable instrument, to map a hardware frontier rigorously,
certify where human design is already optimal, and name the open ground worth pursuing. We
apply it to one stubborn bottleneck: the rate tradeoff governing bias-preserving logical gates
on dissipative cat qubits (the engineered-dissipation-to-loss ratio $\kappa_1/\kappa_2$). We
build a three-condition gate metric whose conjunctive structure makes reward-hacking
worthless, validate it against a known hand-designed gate, then run a search given richer
control than any human design and seeded from the human gate. The result is a \emph{kept
negative}: the search does not beat hand-design and, handed a bias-breaking degree of freedom,
sets it to exactly zero of its own accord. We map the tolerable $\kappa_1/\kappa_2$ frontier,
survey where AI has and has not yet been engaged, and identify the indicated next attack---%
letting AI design the engineered dissipation in time, rather than the drive. We claim
\emph{no novel physics}; the contribution is methodological, and every quantitative claim is
regenerated by in-repository scripts. This is Chapter~2 of an open dossier; it builds on
Chapter~1~\cite{chapter1}. \emph{Don't trust this paper; run it.}
\end{abstract}

\section{AI is already a co-author of quantum computing research}
A qubit is the most error-prone object humanity has tried to compute with, and increasingly
the discipline of keeping it usable is one AI participates in directly---across every layer of
the stack. It \emph{spots the errors}: a transformer-based neural decoder (AlphaQubit) learns
to read syndrome data and outperforms the best hand-built decoders on real
hardware~\cite{alphaqubit}. It \emph{invents the codes}: reinforcement learning searching
bosonic encodings discovered a non-obvious code, built from Fock states $\lvert 2\rangle$ and
$\lvert 4\rangle$, that suppresses single-photon loss past break-even~\cite{rlcode}, and a 2025
successor discovered codes resisting single- and double-photon loss at once~\cite{rlbos}. It
\emph{choreographs the gates} (deep reinforcement learning and optimal control for qubit
pulses) and \emph{designs the hardware's reflexes} (machine-discovered engineered dissipation;
differentiable device models). The field has crossed, without much announcement, from ``can AI
help build a quantum computer?'' to ``AI is already helping, across the stack.''

\section{Three modes of help, and the one this chapter adds}
AI's contributions divide into three modes. Two are widely celebrated---\emph{re-deriving}
human designs and \emph{discovering} new ones. This chapter demonstrates the third:
\emph{scoping}. A search, given an instrument that cannot be fooled, can map a frontier,
certify where human design already sits at the optimum, and point at the open ground. The
counter-intuitive premise is that a careful, honestly-obtained \emph{negative} is among the
most useful outputs a search can produce: it is a map of which doors are already shut, so
effort flows to those still open.

\section{The bottleneck: operating a protected qubit}
Chapter~1~\cite{chapter1} established the floor: a dissipative cat qubit whose engineered
two-photon dissipation exponentially biases noise toward phase errors~\cite{mirrahimi}, an
excellent place to \emph{store} a qubit. The next question is whether one can \emph{operate}
on it without breaking that protection. A bias-preserving gate must be run adiabatically to
remain protected, but adiabaticity gives single-photon loss time to accumulate as phase error.
The result is a quantitative wall expressed as one ratio, $\kappa_1/\kappa_2$ (loss rate over
engineered-dissipation rate): below-threshold operation requires $\kappa_1/\kappa_2 \lesssim
5\times10^{-3}$ at $\alpha^2=8$, with hand-designed optima near $10^{-3}$~\cite{gautier,xu},
and the largest known lever---squeezing the dissipator---buying one to two orders of
magnitude~\cite{squeezedcat}. The bias-preserving gate \emph{set} already exists without
magic-state distillation~\cite{puri}; the open quantitative question is the \emph{rate}.

\section{An instrument that cannot be fooled}
Chapter~1's sharpest lesson was a reward-hacking failure: a naive search improved a protection
score by destroying the qubit. We therefore judge a candidate gate by three conditions that
must hold \emph{simultaneously}---a conjunction, never a weighted sum, so no condition can be
traded against another. A gate must (i) perform the intended logical map; (ii) preserve the
bit-flip bias, measured directly via parity-resolved error rates rather than a lumpable score;
and (iii) remain converged within the code manifold, with a truncation rail rejecting
under-resolved runs. Validated against the known hand-designed dissipative-cat $Z$ gate, the
metric reproduces textbook behaviour (Fig.~\ref{fig:metric}): exponential bit-flip suppression,
linear phase-flip growth, and frontier degradation near $\kappa_1/\kappa_2 \approx
5\times10^{-3}$. This validates the \emph{measurement} against known physics before any search
optimises against it (ledger G01).

\section{The search, and the kept negative}
We optimise piecewise-constant two-quadrature drive pulses for a $Z(\pi/2)$ gate using a
max--min objective---maximising the \emph{worst} normalised margin across the three
conditions---which is conjunction-faithful and forbids trading bias for fidelity. A bake-off of
two search arms (gradient-free differential evolution and finite-difference L-BFGS) agreed
exactly, so differential evolution is the workhorse. The search was given more freedom than any
hand design (two quadratures, finer segmentation, free gate time) and \emph{seeded with the
hand-designed gate}, so it began from the human answer and could only improve.

It did not. Across cat sizes $\alpha^2\in\{2,3\}$ (fully searched), the search recovered the
hand-designed gate and could not push the tolerable $\kappa_1/\kappa_2$ past it (frontier gain
$\approx\times1.04$, consistent with noise; independently reproduced). The hand-designed
adiabatic gate, run as fast as it can go, already sits at the control-space optimum
(Fig.~\ref{fig:negative}). The diagnostic detail: handed a second quadrature that could speed
the gate by breaking the bias, the search drove its amplitude to \emph{exactly zero}---because
the un-gameable metric makes bias-breaking worthless, cheating was not punished after the fact
but rendered pointless, and the optimiser found that on its own. The mapped frontier
(Fig.~\ref{fig:frontier}) gives tolerable $\kappa_1/\kappa_2 = \{5.17, 3.99, 3.19\}\times10^{-3}$
at $\alpha^2=\{2,3,4\}$ (converged truncation), monotone decreasing (ledger G02, G03).

\noindent\emph{Scope, stated plainly.} Single-mode $Z(\pi/2)$; $\alpha^2\in\{2,3\}$ fully
searched, $\alpha^2=4$ baseline-only (compute-bound at honest truncation); two-quadrature drive
shaping; one validity bar. Not CNOT, not large cats, not dissipation-schedule shaping. The one
untried control axis---shaping the engineered dissipation in time---is the indicated next step.

\section{Where AI has not yet been engaged}
Genuine white space is narrow and shrinking, itself evidence for the thesis: even cross-layer
co-design is now being engaged by AI (jointly learning encodings and logical gate
sets~\cite{vareft}). The verified-thin gaps are specific: AI that shapes the engineered
dissipation \emph{in time} (today's schemes pair static dissipation with a shaped drive); AI
that designs dissipation for \emph{gates} rather than for memory; and AI used as an adversarial
\emph{referee}---to certify protection and hunt reward-hacks---rather than only as an optimiser,
the mode this chapter's metric embodies. Candidate (not adversarially cleared) white space:
AI discovering qualitatively new confinement Lindbladians; end-to-end co-design of the
biased-noise stack; and closed-loop AI on real cat hardware.

\section{What this says about AI}
Chapter~1's pivotal moment was a failure caught and kept: a search reward-hacking its own
metric, reported rather than hidden. This chapter is the same virtue one level up. There, AI
caught itself cheating on a \emph{metric}; here, given a real frontier and a tempting shortcut,
AI \emph{declined} the cheat unprompted, mapped the wall, certified the human design optimal,
and pointed where to push next. Re-deriving and discovering attract the headlines; scoping---%
knowing where the wall is and saying so---is the quieter capability that makes a search
trustworthy.

\section{Next: design the dissipation, not the pulse}
The limit is a conjunction: a protected gate must be slow, \emph{and} slowness exposes loss.
The search broke neither clause because it tuned only the drive. The indicated move changes the
\emph{object} the AI designs---the protective dissipation itself, made time-dependent---rather
than the pulse atop it, and it runs on the instrument already built and shipped by swapping the
search variable from the drive to the jump operators. A learned, gate-synchronised squeezing
schedule~\cite{squeezedcat} is the concrete candidate. This is posted as the challenge for
Chapter~3, with named credit for whoever closes it. Robotics---automating the
build--measure--learn loop on real devices---is the program's other arm, named in the series
title and reserved for a later chapter; it enters when the work moves from simulation onto
hardware (ledger G04, G05).
